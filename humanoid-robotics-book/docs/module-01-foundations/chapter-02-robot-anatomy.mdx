# Chapter 2: Robot Anatomy

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, you will:
- Identify the main components of every robot
- Understand how different robot types differ in structure
- Learn about power systems and their role in robotics
- Understand the basic robot chassis types and their applications

## Introduction

Just as biologists study animal anatomy to understand how living things work, roboticists must understand robot anatomy to design and build effective robots. Every robot, from a simple vacuum to a humanoid, shares fundamental components organized in similar ways.

## Basic Robot Components

All robots share five core components:

### 1. **Chassis (Structure)**
The robot's body or frame that holds everything together.
- Provides mechanical strength
- Determines the robot's size, shape, and form factor
- Must be lightweight but sturdy
- Examples: Metal frame, plastic body, aluminum exoskeleton

### 2. **Actuators (Movement)**
Devices that create motion and apply force.
- Electric motors are most common in robotics
- Types: DC motors, servo motors, stepper motors
- Hydraulic and pneumatic actuators used in heavy-duty applications
- Enable the robot to move, manipulate, and interact

### 3. **Sensors (Perception)**
Devices that detect information about the environment.
- Vision sensors (cameras)
- Distance sensors (ultrasonic, LiDAR)
- Touch sensors (pressure, force)
- Motion sensors (accelerometers, gyroscopes)
- Environmental sensors (temperature, humidity)

### 4. **Control System (Brain)**
The computer and electronics that make decisions.
- Microcontroller or full computer
- Receives sensor data
- Processes information
- Sends commands to actuators
- Examples: Arduino, Raspberry Pi, NVIDIA Jetson

### 5. **Power System (Energy)**
Batteries or power supplies that energize everything.
- Rechargeable lithium batteries most common
- Power consumption depends on actuators used
- Runtime limited by battery capacity
- Must account for voltage requirements of different components

## Robot Chassis Types

Different robot types use different chassis designs optimized for their environment and task:

### **Wheeled Robots**
- **Advantages**: Fast, efficient, simple mechanics
- **Disadvantages**: Limited on rough terrain, can't climb stairs
- **Uses**: Vacuum cleaners, delivery robots, warehouse robots
- **Examples**: iRobot Roomba, AMR (Autonomous Mobile Robots)

### **Legged Robots**
- **Advantages**: Can traverse complex terrain, climb stairs, navigate obstacles
- **Disadvantages**: Complex mechanics, energy-intensive, slower
- **Uses**: Inspection, search and rescue, entertainment
- **Examples**: Boston Dynamics Spot (4-legged), ASIMO (humanoid)

### **Aerial Robots (Drones)**
- **Advantages**: Access to elevated areas, wide coverage, speed
- **Disadvantages**: Limited flight time, weather-dependent, regulatory restrictions
- **Uses**: Delivery, surveillance, photography, mapping
- **Examples**: DJI Phantom, commercial drones

### **Manipulators (Robotic Arms)**
- **Advantages**: High precision, strong grip, flexible movement
- **Disadvantages**: Stationary unless on mobile base, limited workspace
- **Uses**: Manufacturing, assembly, surgery, research
- **Examples**: ABB Industrial Arms, Collaborative Robots (Cobots)

### **Hybrid Systems**
- Combine multiple locomotion types for maximum flexibility
- Example: Mobile manipulator = wheeled base + robotic arm
- Used in modern manufacturing and research

## Power Systems in Robotics

### Battery Chemistry and Types

**Lithium-Ion (Li-Ion)**
- Most popular in modern robots
- Good energy density, reasonable cost
- Used in: Drones, mobile robots, consumer electronics

**Lead-Acid**
- Older technology, heavier
- Good for stationary industrial robots
- Lower cost but limited lifespan

**Nickel-Metal Hydride (NiMH)**
- Safer than Li-Ion but lower energy density
- Used in some educational robots

### Power Considerations

Every robot designer must balance:
- **Runtime**: How long can the robot operate?
- **Weight**: Heavier batteries add mass (especially problematic for aerial robots)
- **Cost**: Battery can be 20-30% of robot cost
- **Charging Time**: How quickly can it recharge for next deployment?
- **Safety**: Proper thermal management to prevent fires

## Did You Know?

Boston Dynamics' Spot has a battery that lasts about 3 hours of continuous operation with a single charge. The team had to carefully design the robot's mechanical and electrical systems to maximize efficiency and runtime.

## Key Takeaways

- **Five Core Components**: Chassis, Actuators, Sensors, Control System, Power System
- **Chassis Types**: Wheeled (fast, simple), Legged (agile, complex), Aerial (fast, limited), Manipulators (precise, stationary)
- **Actuators Enable Motion**: Motors convert electrical energy into mechanical movement
- **Power is Critical**: Battery capacity and efficiency determine robot runtime
- **Integrated Design**: All components must work together harmoniously
